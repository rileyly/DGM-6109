Using sunny days to explore new places keeps me engaged with photography.

Research Report

Research Question

This project explores whether taking more walks inspires me to take more photos.

Hypothesis: As the number of steps I take increases, the number of photos I take increases.

Second factor: Rainy conditions may negatively affect how much I walk and/or how many photos I take.

Third factor: I also hypothesize that I take more photos when visiting new, unfamiliar places than when I spend time familiar ones (e.g. my own neighbourhood).

Data Overview

Basic Data

  • Date
  • Number of steps walked
  • Number of photos taken

Weather

  • Amount of precipitation (mm)
  • Temperature (C; high, low)

Personal

  • Energy level (1-10; am, pm)

Location Data

  • Number of locations visited
  • List of locations visited
  • Level of familiarity with location visited (1-5)
  • Average daily level of familiarity
  • Maximum distance traveled from home (km)
  • Did I visit a park? (true/false)
  • Did I visit a shopping corridor? (true/false)
  • Did I travel to campus for class? (true/false)

Travel Method

  • Did I travel via bus? (true/false)
  • Did I travel via Skytrain? (true/false)

The bare minimum data I needed to answer my question were: date, steps, and photos.

I included weather data because I was curious if outdoor conditions (like rain, snow, extremes of heat and cold) would play a role in how much I walk.

My data covers a daily time scale. However, data not subject to memory decay (e.g. max distance, photo count, boolean variables) were logged retroactively, on an approximately weekly basis.

Conclusion

Based on my visualization, it does appear that walking more leads to more photos.

On rainy days, not only am I less likely to take photos, I'm less likely to leave the house except as required.

I also tend to surpass my average photo count when visiting locations with which I am less familiar.
